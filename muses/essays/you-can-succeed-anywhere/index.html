You can succeed anywhere

Published on Fri May 01 2020

You can succeed anywhere if you...

Have these things

self-awareness

awareness of what you like and don't like (at the very least)

desire/hunger

do you want it badly enough?

resilience

can you stand up after every fall?

flexibility

when circumstances change, can you pivot fast enough?

Do these things

pursue your own definition of success.

you'll be happier and more motivated to achieve it.

take action towards your dream

if you don't take a step forward, you will stay where you are.

Why do I say this?

Years ago, I had scored near-straight A1s for my GCE "O" Levels (secondary school leaving examination). I had two choices for my post-secondary education:

Junior College (2 years)

Equivalent to a high school where I'd learn subjects like Chemistry, Physics and Math, mostly from an academic, theoretical perspective

Polytechnic (3 years)

Equivalent to a vocational school where I'd learn the ins and outs of a trade, mostly through a hands-on, skills-based approach

My family members wanted me to pursue my degree as soon as possible, so they urged me to go to junior college.

My teachers felt that I'd "be in better company" in a junior college than a polytechnic.

On 17 April 2017, despite objections from everyone around me, I started my freshman year at Nanyang Polytechnic.

Why did I "rebel"?

I was blessed with self-awareness

I knew that I wanted to pursue either/both Digital Design and IT - subjects that I'll never touch on in junior college.

I knew that I learnt best through practicals/hands-on sessions.

I knew that the mastery of design and IT is acquired with practice, not pure theory.

I knew that I am a consistent worker that can score well for day-to-assignments - a trait that allows me to do better in the scoring system of a polytechnic than a junior college.

Unlike graduating from polytechnic, I would have no monetisable skills after I graduate from junior college. Thus, I would have no other option but to retake GCE "A" Levels/go to private university if I didn't score well.

And I detest the idea of having one exam deciding my entire fate.

Someone else had done it too

Because of how our education system is structured, most polytechnics students were in the poorer-scoring portion of the cohort for the national exams.

As such, a deep-rooted bias against polytechnic students formed in the entire country: Since their results are so bad, polytechnic students must be the kind of people who "don't want study".

I was explicitly judged as such by a taxi driver, after he found out that I was a polytechnic student during our friendly conversation.

But I knew that there are a thousand factors that could pull down someone's academic performance - factors beyond their control and they had to make do with. They may not have the resources to afford private tuition and get more help. They may not have a conducive household to study in after school.

And then in 2016, the year I was due to take my "O" Levels, an alumnus of Nanyang Polytechnic was accepted by four of the top colleges in the US - Harvard, MIT, Carnegie Mellon University and New York University.

He had scored seven As at the "O" levels.

If studying in polytechnic was such a terrible choice like that taxi driver had described, this alumnus of Nanyang Polytechnic wouldn't exist. A desire in me started to form: I wanted to prove it to my family members (at least) that they were wrong about polytechnic students... and that I can succeed in any endeavour I choose to pursue.

And thus, I rebelled.

I set my own definition of success

It was a simple 3-year plan that consisted of outcomes:

Graduate at the top of my cohort with straight distinctions and the Lee Kuan Yew Distinguished Award

Score straight As for my Diploma Plus modules

Be a Worldskills Singapore 2018 Gold Medallist (Web Design and Development)

Be WorldSkills Kazan 2019 Gold Medallist (Web Design and Development)

Be a WorldSkills Kazan 2019 Albert Vidal Awardee

I took action towards my dreams

I kept my radar on for WorldSkills selections since the first day of school.

I told my would-be coaches that I was willing to learn - my lack of background in programming, let alone web design and development, would not be an issue.

Even though WorldSkills training became tougher and tougher as I progressed, I persisted and held on.

Eventually, I had to juggle training and maintaining my GPA - and I never felt that it was a huge struggle. I focused on chipping away at each task, chunk by chunk, and got things done.

I fell... face flat. A lot of times.

My first fall was at the ASEANSkills Competition in 2018.

Out of 7 competitors from my school, 5 of us had a Gold/Silver/Bronze medal or a Medallion of Excellence.

I was one of the 2 competitors who came home empty-handed.

Imagine being a national champion, only to be told that you suck on the world stage. That really hurt.

Back then, I was still a "brooding hen" - I instinctively dwell and wallow on shortcomings.

If there was a black dot on a piece of white paper, I would focus on the black dot and how it ruined the paper, instead of appreciating the whiteness around it. As such, I had trouble recovering form my ASEANSkills results.

My coaches knew this, so they allowed me to take a break from WorldSkills for a while. But they kept reminding me that ultimately, only my performance at WorldSkills Kazan 2019 matters. It's better to fail now and learn from your mistakes... than to fail at your final competition in Kazan.

Every second I wasted on wallowing could've be used for training.

Slowly, I learnt how to stand up quickly after each fall.

I went for Digital Skills, a friendly match held by WorldSkills Russia. I failed a paper and barely passed another.

I went for my final friendly match in Guangzhou, where I ranked 4th out of 6 competitors and was 3 points away from a Medallion of Excellence again.

With my coaches' help, I learnt how to these failures in my stride and started seeing them as learning checkpoints instead of mere "events that happened in my life".

I kept my eye on the prize - WorldSkills Kazan 2019. And then finally, competition day came.

I did not do well for my second day of competition. I knew it in my heart, from the trashy way my website and API looked.

I had not been flexible enough to pivot my abilities towards the competition tasks.

At the end of my second day, my coaches saw my face and gave me "that look". I steeled myself and snapped out of it over dinner, focusing on the next day's tasks after a quick shower.

I only allowed myself to cry about the trash and mess and opportunities I've wasted after the last day of competition.

As I later found out, being able to snap out of it quickly saved me from scoring worse. Being able to shift my focus away from the desired outcome, to instead focus on the process of achieving those outcomes, had helped.

Eventually, I ranked 5th out of the 32 countries that participated, which is Singapore's best ranking in the Web Technologies trade thus far.

As someone who had zero programming knowledge only two years prior, I am grateful for how things had turned out.

Yes, I didn't achieve all of my goals.

I didn't manage to score straight distinctions - I had one A.

I never took a single Diploma Plus module, nor did I even win a Bronze medal at WorldSkills Kazan 2019 - the Albert Vidal Award was out of the question.

But I did become a WorldSkills Singapore Gold Medallist. I did graduate at the top of my cohort. And in the pursuit of my goals, I was exposed to so many opportunities to learn and grow.

And because of that, I had proven to my family - more importantly, myself - that I can achieve success in whatever I set my mind towards.

Sure, I'm still learning about how to be more self-aware, hungry, resilient and flexible.

But 1% growth every day is better than no growth at all.

You can succeed anywhere.

If you don't know what you want, explore your options.

If you're still considering your options, my advice to you would be that the right choice is the most suitable choice.

If you've already made your choice, my advice to you would be to play full out. 10X your goals, as I did with mine. Aim to be the best at what you do... and then take action to be the best at what you do.

If you fall, get up as quickly as you can. Make the most of what you have and work actively to get back on track.

One day, you'll look back and realise how far you've come.

To your success,

Tea
