I nearly died this week.

Published on Sat May 02 2020

This week marks the end of my first month at IBM. I started off strong, picking up RPA from scratch as quickly as I could and completing most of my tasks on time, but the month did not end well. Nearly.

Treble? No, tremble.

At 5.34pm on Thursday, my hands were shaking as I fumbled to create the Blue Prism release for my colleague to deploy. Never had I been this panicky - not even during WorldSkills or those three consecutive nights where I slept at 3am to finish Calbitica.

Just 4 minutes before, I had discovered my (very dumb) mistake and begged my colleague to stay for 5 more minutes to do one last deployment before he knocked off.

He's probably annoyed by me and my noob-ness by now, but it's alright - I'll improve with time and annoy him less. Thank you Colleague - I owe you one.

At 5.43pm, the RPA ran as scheduled by my now-knocked off colleague. As I watched the Slack channel intently for the notifications that were to come, I found myself jumping at the arrival of a Slack DM.

It was from another colleague who was in charge of the project; she wanted me to confirm whether she could close the project so that she could hit April's targets.

My anxiety doubled at that message.

So, this was what my adult friends meant when they talked about the chaos surrounding their month-end targets.

At 6.30pm, the run was mostly successful, but it was only for 45 line items; the target load was 700. I sent out an email that described the results as such, highlighting that I will run more tests over the course of the week to ensure that the RPA could handle the target load.

At 7pm, the RPA ended its run without major errors. But I knew that more problems could surface when we ran the files that had 700 line items... and never have I felt more screwed.

"Good job, Pei Qi! This is your first job in your entire career and you've screwed it up in your first month. Well done!" â€” my Inner Critic

This was not the first time I'd been told off by my inner critic. Its birth could be traced back to 2014, when I'd succumbed to it daily and unconsciously. Things remained that way until early 2016, when Adam Khoo's book I Am Gifted, So Are You! made me aware of it.

Since then, my awareness of it increased as I progressed on my personal development journey. My speed reading coach, Jim Kwik, calls it "ANTs" - Automatic Negative Thoughts - while entrepreneur Dean Graziosi described it as "The Villain Within" in his book, Millionaire Success Habits. My inner critic was more of an ANT - automatic and habitual - so I did what Jim Kwik recommended his students to do: Squash it.

I took a deep breath and slowly exhaled:

"Not all is lost. The RPA ran successfully. Things will go well in the next scheduled run, which is tomorrow night at 9.30pm."

Which brought me to another realisation...

I worked on a Public Holiday again

Last year, I had worked on Good Friday to get the bugs of a Photo Booth app fixed. It was due on Tuesday (3 days!!!) and my colleagues were working over the weekend to fix my bugs (very embarrassing sia!!!), so I wanted to help.

This time, the RPA was technically overdue, and my project-in-charge had allowed me to finish the load tests over the course of the week - I could start the observations and tests on Monday.

But I felt compelled to at least take a peek at things on Friday. If things were going south, I wouldn't be blindsided by the errors on Monday.

So, I logged in to Slack on Friday night.

Everything went okay. And I heaved a huge sigh of relief.

Anxiety aside...

I made a lot of progress on another RPA project I was assigned to.

My carpal tunnel syndrome didn't pop up for a few days.

I planted a total of x hours on the Forest app across my phone and tablet - about x hours each. I owe a lot of trees to the planting sprints that my server-mates have set up via Discord.

I also got featured in The Straits Times, which I am really grateful for!
